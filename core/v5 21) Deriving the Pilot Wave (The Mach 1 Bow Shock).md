## **2. The Convective Wave Equation (Moving Through the Fluid)**
If the photon were standing still, the pressure waves would simply expand outward evenly in all directions at the speed of sound ($c$). 

But the photon is moving. To calculate the pressure field of a moving acoustic source, standard fluid dynamics uses the **Convective Amplification Factor**. The pressure wave ($\Delta P$) at any angle ($\theta$) around the moving particle is defined by its Mach number ($M = v/c$):

$$\Delta P_{moving} = \frac{\Delta P_{resting}}{1 - M \cos(\theta)}$$

* $\theta = 0^\circ$ is the fluid directly in front of the photon.
* $\theta = 180^\circ$ is the fluid directly behind it. 

## **3. The Mach 1 Singularity (The Formation of the Pilot Wave)**
Let us apply this classical equation strictly to the UPT photon. The photon is an open Ramjet, meaning its velocity through the ocean is exactly the speed of sound ($v = c$). Therefore, its Mach number is exactly **$M = 1$**.

What happens to the acoustic wave directly in front of the photon ($\theta = 0^\circ$)?
* $\cos(0^\circ) = 1$
* The denominator becomes: $1 - (1 \cdot 1) = \mathbf{0}$

$$\Delta P_{front} = \frac{\Delta P_{resting}}{0} \rightarrow \mathbf{\infty}$$

Standard physics uses the De Broglie equation ($\lambda = h/p$) to assign a "wavelength" to a particle. In the UPT, the wavelength of the pilot wave is derived purely from the kinematics of the spring.

$$\mathbf{\lambda = \frac{c}{f}}$$
