$$\text{Charge Wake} = (\text{Total Wire Length}) \times (\text{Pitch Efficiency})$$

To maintain charge equality between the Electron ($e$) and the Proton ($p$):
$$L_p \cdot \alpha_p = L_e \cdot \alpha_e$$

Substitute the Mass-Length Identity ($L_p = 1836 \cdot L_e$):
$$(1836 \cdot L_e) \cdot \alpha_p = L_e \cdot \alpha_e$$

Cancel the electron length from both sides to reveal the absolute geometric lock:
$$1836 \cdot \alpha_p = \alpha_e$$
$$\mathbf{\alpha_p = \frac{\alpha_e}{1836}}$$

**The Geometric Result:** If the Electron's pitch coefficient is $\approx 0.00729$ (an angle of $\approx 4.9^\circ$), the massive Proton must be wound almost perfectly flat to avoid generating too much charge. Its pitch coefficient is a microscopic $\mathbf{0.00000397}$ (an angle of $\mathbf{\approx 0.11^\circ}$).

## **3. The Neutron ($\alpha_n$): The Helical Torsion Equation**

To calculate the Neutron's pitch, we must use the geometry of a coiled helix. A helix forms a right triangle where the hypotenuse is the physical wire ($L_{pipe}$ or Total Mass), and the base is the macroscopic path of the knot ($L_{base}$). 

Using the Pythagorean relationship $\cos(\theta) = L_{base} / L_{pipe}$ and knowing that $\sin^2(\theta) = \alpha$, we derive the fundamental UPT Mass Equation:
$$L_{pipe} = \frac{L_{base}}{\sqrt{1 - \alpha}}$$

$$Mass_{neutron} = \frac{Mass_{proton}}{\sqrt{1 - \alpha_n}}$$

Solve for the Neutron's specific pitch angle:
$$\sqrt{1 - \alpha_n} = \frac{M_{proton}}{M_{neutron}}$$
$$1 - \alpha_n = \left(\frac{M_{proton}}{M_{neutron}}\right)^2$$
$$\mathbf{\alpha_n = 1 - \left(\frac{M_{proton}}{M_{neutron}}\right)^2}$$

**The Geometric Result:** Plugging in the real-world mass values ($\approx 938.27 / 939.56$) yields a pitch efficiency of **$\mathbf{\alpha_n \approx 0.00275}$**.
